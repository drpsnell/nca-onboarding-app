# Case Study: The Weekend Warrior

**Difficulty:** Intermediate | **Region:** Lower Quarter

**Primary Concepts:** Disc Herniation, Radiculopathy, McKenzie, Directional Preference, Hip Hinge, Patient Agency

---

## Patient Presentation

### Demographics
- **Age:** 42 years old
- **Sex:** Male
- **Occupation:** Accountant

### Chief Complaint
Right-sided low back pain radiating to the posterior thigh, present for 3 weeks.

### History of Present Illness
Mr. Thompson is a 42-year-old accountant who presents with right-sided low back pain that began 3 weeks ago after helping a friend move furniture. He describes lifting a heavy dresser with a rounded back when he felt a 'pop' in his lower back followed by immediate sharp pain. Initially the pain was localized to his low back, but over the following week it began radiating down the posterior aspect of his right thigh to just above the knee.

The pain is worse with sitting, forward bending, and first thing in the morning. He reports the pain improves somewhat when walking and when lying on his left side with a pillow between his knees. He rates the pain 7/10 at worst and describes it as a deep ache with occasional sharp shooting sensations down the leg.

He denies bowel or bladder changes, saddle anesthesia, or bilateral leg symptoms. He has been taking ibuprofen 600mg TID with minimal relief.

### Past Medical History
- Hypertension - controlled with lisinopril
- Two prior episodes of low back pain (5 years ago, 2 years ago) - both resolved with rest and NSAIDs within 2 weeks
- No prior spine imaging
- No surgeries

### Social History
- **Occupation:** Desk job, sits 8-10 hours/day
- **Exercise:** Plays recreational basketball on weekends, occasional gym workouts
- **Smoking:** Never
- **Alcohol:** Social, 2-3 beers on weekends

### Medications & Allergies
- **Current:** Lisinopril 10mg daily, Ibuprofen 600mg TID PRN
- **Allergies:** NKDA

## Socratic Questions

### Question 1: Red Flag Screening
**Phase:** Assessment

**Question:** Based on the history, what red flags should you specifically screen for in this patient? Name at least 3 and explain why they are relevant.

<details>
<summary>Hints</summary>

- Consider the age and onset pattern
- What symptoms would indicate cauda equina syndrome?
- What in his history might warrant imaging?
</details>

<details>
<summary>Expected Concepts</summary>

- **Cauda equina syndrome** (saddle anesthesia, bowel/bladder dysfunction, bilateral symptoms) - patient denies all
- **Progressive neurological deficit** - motor testing intact
- **Cancer** (though less likely at age 42 without red flag history)
- **Infection** (no fever, no risk factors reported)
</details>

> **Teaching Point:** Red flag screening should be performed on every patient with spinal pain. While this patient has radicular symptoms, he denies cauda equina signs. The mechanism of injury (lifting) and pattern of symptoms are consistent with mechanical etiology rather than sinister pathology.

### Question 2: Graded Neuro Exam (GNE)
**Phase:** Assessment

**Question:** Organize your neurological findings using the Graded Neuro Exam (GNE) framework. What does each level tell you?

<details>
<summary>Hints</summary>

- Start with brain-level factors from history
- What does the slump test tell you about the spinal cord/nerve root/IVF level?
- What do the skin rolling findings suggest?
</details>

<details>
<summary>Expected Concepts</summary>

- **Brain:** Prior episodes resolved - no catastrophizing evident, appropriate health-seeking behavior, realistic expectations
- **Cellular:** Sedentary lifestyle, high-stress occupation may contribute to inflammatory load
- **Spinal cord/nerve root/IVF:** Positive slump and SLR with cervical modulation indicates nerve root involvement, likely L5 or S1 based on distribution
- **Peripheral nerves:** Sciatic tract involvement demonstrated by tension tests
- **Cutaneous:** Hyperalgesia on skin rolling suggests sensitized superficial neurology in affected region
</details>

> **Teaching Point:** The GNE organizes our neurological assessment from bigger to smaller structures. This patient shows involvement at the nerve root level (positive tension tests with proximal modulation) and secondary cutaneous sensitization.

### Question 3: Neurocentric Lens & Patient Narrative
**Phase:** Conceptualization

**Question:** Contrast the traditional 'mesodermal lens' versus 'neurocentric lens'. Then, how would you explain to Mr. Thompson WHY his back became painful in language he can understand?

<details>
<summary>Hints</summary>

- What would a purely structural/mechanical diagnosis focus on?
- How does the NCA framework reframe the tight paraspinals?
- How would you use analogy to explain disc mechanics to a patient?
</details>

<details>
<summary>Expected Concepts</summary>

**Mesodermal lens:**
- Disc herniation causing mechanical compression
- Tight muscles are the problem requiring release
- Joint dysfunction requiring manipulation

**Neurocentric lens:**
- Nerve root is mechanically sensitized (reduced perfusion from compression)
- Paraspinal hypertonicity is protective (muscles protect nerves)
- Need to reduce threat to neurology rather than force muscle relaxation
- NCA approach focuses on reducing neural threat through positioning/movement (McKenzie) before aggressive soft tissue work

**Patient Narrative Examples:**
- *"The disc between your vertebrae is like a jelly donut. When you bent forward with that heavy dresser, the jelly got pushed toward the back where the nerve lives. That's why bending forward hurts and makes your leg symptoms worse."*
- *"The good news is we can use extension exercises to push the jelly back toward the center. Your body is already trying to protect itself - those tight back muscles are guarding the irritated nerve. That's actually helpful right now."*
- *"Think of the exercises like medicine - we're going to dose them throughout the day to keep that disc material where it belongs."*
</details>

> **Teaching Point:** The patient needs a mental model for WHY the exercises work. The 'jelly donut' analogy helps them understand disc mechanics intuitively. Explaining that their tight muscles are protective (not the enemy) reframes their experience and builds trust. This narrative gives them agency - they understand cause and effect.

### Question 4: Working Diagnosis
**Phase:** Diagnosis

**Question:** What is your working diagnosis? How does the McKenzie response inform your clinical reasoning?

<details>
<summary>Hints</summary>

- What does peripheralization indicate?
- What does centralization indicate?
- What is the directional preference?
</details>

<details>
<summary>Expected Concepts</summary>

- **Working diagnosis:** Lumbar disc herniation with right L5 or S1 radiculopathy
- **Peripheralization with flexion** confirms disc as pain generator (flexion increases intradiscal pressure and posteriorly displaces nuclear material)
- **Centralization with extension** indicates extension IS the directional preference - this is the KEY finding
- **Centralization** is a strong positive prognostic indicator for conservative care
- **Negative Laslett cluster** (0/6 tests positive) rules out SI joint as primary pain generator
- The combination of centralizing symptoms + negative SI cluster strongly supports discogenic origin
</details>

> **Teaching Point:** The McKenzie response is diagnostic and prognostic. When symptoms centralize with repeated extension, extension IS the directional preference - that's the home exercise. Don't overcomplicate with lateral components unless extension alone fails to centralize. The negative Laslett SI cluster helps rule out SI joint involvement.

### Question 5: Clinical Audit Process (CAP) & Patient Agency
**Phase:** Treatment

**Question:** Design a treatment plan using the Clinical Audit Process (CAP). How will you demonstrate immediate symptom modification to build patient agency?

<details>
<summary>Hints</summary>

- What is the index movement?
- How do you demonstrate cause-and-effect to the patient?
- What is the sequence of interventions based on GNE levels?
</details>

<details>
<summary>Expected Concepts</summary>

- **Index movement:** Forward bending (tying shoes) - 7/10 pain
- **CRITICAL:** Demonstrate symptom modification IN THE VISIT - this builds patient agency
- **CAP demonstration:** Perform 10 prone press-ups → immediately retest forward bending → patient experiences reduction to 2/10
- Patient sees cause-and-effect: *"When I do this exercise, my pain decreases"* - this develops locus of control
- The patient's surprised response (*"I can't believe that worked"*) is the moment of buy-in
- **Sequence if needed:** Address nerve root level first (McKenzie), then peripheral (neurodynamics), then cutaneous (DTM)
- **Manual therapy:** Consider manipulation after directional preference confirmed, DTM to hyperalgesic areas after neural threat reduced
</details>

> **Teaching Point:** The within-visit symptom modification demonstration is TRANSFORMATIVE. When Mr. Thompson bends forward at 7/10, does his press-ups, then bends forward at 2/10 - he now believes he can control his pain. This is patient agency. Without this demonstration, you're asking him to trust exercises he hasn't seen work.

### Question 6: Home Exercise & Functional Training
**Phase:** Home Exercise

**Question:** What home exercise prescription would you provide? Include directional preference exercises, specific ADL modifications, and the functional training progression for correcting faulty movement patterns.

<details>
<summary>Hints</summary>

- How do you dose the directional preference exercise?
- What specific ADLs need modification and how?
- What is the progression for retraining forward bending and lifting?
</details>

<details>
<summary>Expected Concepts</summary>

**DIRECTIONAL PREFERENCE:**
- Prone press-ups 10 reps every 1-2 hours while awake - dose like medication
- Morning routine: Cat/Camel mobilizations before getting out of bed (the spine is more hydrated and vulnerable in AM)

**ADL MODIFICATIONS:**
| Activity | Modification |
|----------|--------------|
| Brushing teeth | Hip hinge at sink, not spinal flexion - "nose over toes, butt back" |
| Picking up objects | Golfer's pick-up (one leg back) or reverse lunge for light items |
| Putting on shoes | Sit down or use long-handled shoe horn - no flexed spine loading |
| Getting out of chair | Nose over toes, push through heels, maintain lordosis |
| Toilet | Use armrests to lower/rise, maintain lordosis |

**FUNCTIONAL TRAINING PROGRESSION (Correct Faulty Movement):**

| Phase | Exercise | Cues |
|-------|----------|------|
| **Phase 1** | Hip Hinge | "Chest up, butt back, weight on heels." Use dowel on spine to monitor neutral. This replaces flexion-based bending. |
| **Phase 2** | Box Squat | Two fingers on L3-5 to monitor lordosis. "Shirt with name on it stays visible" (chest up). Arms parallel to floor for counterbalance. Squat to box/chair height. |
| **Phase 3** | Sumo Deadlift | Wide stance, toes out, chest up, butt back, weight on heels, active triceps. This retrains safe lifting pattern for return to activity. |

**SELF-MONITORING:**
- If symptoms peripheralize during activity, stop and perform press-ups
- If symptoms persist peripherally after press-ups, reduce activity and contact provider
</details>

> **Teaching Point:** Home exercise has two components: (1) Directional preference exercises dosed throughout the day for symptom management, and (2) Functional training to correct the faulty movement pattern that caused the injury. Mr. Thompson lifted with a rounded back - he needs to learn the hip hinge pattern to replace that. The progression Hip Hinge → Box Squat → Sumo Deadlift retrains safe movement so he doesn't re-injure himself helping the next friend move.

### Question 7: McKenzie Purgatory & NCA Integration
**Phase:** Integration

**Question:** If Mr. Thompson returns in 2 weeks with 50% improvement but has plateaued despite compliant home exercise, what would you consider? How does NCA help you escape 'McKenzie Purgatory'?

<details>
<summary>Hints</summary>

- What other GNE levels might still be involved?
- What would skin rolling tell you at this point?
- When do you add neurodynamics or DTM?
</details>

<details>
<summary>Expected Concepts</summary>

- **McKenzie Purgatory:** Patient responds initially to directional preference but plateaus before full resolution
- McKenzie addresses the IVF/nerve root level but may not fully address peripheral nerve or cutaneous involvement
- **Re-examine with GNE lens:** Is skin rolling still hyperalgesic? Is slump test still positive?
- **If cutaneous hyperalgesia persists:** Add DTM to the hyperalgesic paraspinal and gluteal regions identified on initial exam
- **If neural tension persists:** Add sciatic nerve sliders (not tensioners initially) to improve nerve tract mobility
- **The CAP guides progression:** After DTM or neurodynamics, retest index movement - if further improvement, you've identified a contributing level McKenzie alone didn't address
- **This is the NCA value-add:** McKenzie is powerful but addresses one level. NCA integrates multiple levels for complete resolution.
</details>

> **Teaching Point:** Many patients plateau with McKenzie alone because other neurological levels remain sensitized. The protective paraspinal tension may have created fascial adhesions affecting cutaneous nerves (like cluneal nerves). The disc may have healed but secondary neural sensitivity persists. NCA's multi-level approach - adding DTM for cutaneous, neurodynamics for peripheral nerve - provides escape from McKenzie Purgatory.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Centralization with extension = extension IS the directional preference.** Don't overcomplicate.

2. **Within-visit symptom modification demonstration builds patient agency** - they must SEE it work.

3. **Patient narrative ('jelly donut' analogy)** helps them understand cause-and-effect and take ownership.

4. **ADL modification is essential:** hip hinge at sink, golfer's pick-up, Cat/Camel for AM.

5. **Functional training progression:** Hip Hinge → Box Squat → Sumo Deadlift corrects faulty movement patterns.

6. **McKenzie Purgatory:** If plateau occurs, use GNE to identify other levels (cutaneous, peripheral nerve) and add DTM/neurodynamics.

7. **Paraspinal hypertonicity is protective** - don't aggressively release before reducing neural threat.
